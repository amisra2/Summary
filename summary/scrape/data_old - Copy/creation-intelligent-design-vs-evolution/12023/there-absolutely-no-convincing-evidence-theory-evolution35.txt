           <br/>

           <br/>

           The thing about dichotomies is that you cannot ignore them - you have to fall one side or the other. You can't be just 'an agnostic' - you have to be 'an agnostic'
           <i>
            about
           </i>

           <a href="http://www.businessweek.com/news/2010-02-11/obama-agnostic-on-deficit-cuts-won-t-prejudge-tax-increases.html" target="_blank">

           ). Simple maths, then, dictates that you have a combination of four possibles to choose from:
           <br/>
           <br/>
           <ul>
            <li style="">
             Gnostic theist - one who believes in God, and is certain of it. For example: those who claim to have had a 'personal experience'.
            </li>
            <li style="">
             Agnostic theist - one who believes in God, but cannot be sure. For example: who were born into the faith and don't see any reason to change.
            </li>
            <li style="">
             Gnostic atheist - one who does not believe in God, and is fully confident in this lack of belief. For example: Richard Dawkins, Christopher Hitchens and any 'strong' atheist; the terms are synonymous.
            </li>
            <li style="">
             Agnostic atheist - one who does not believe in God, but accepts that they may be wrong. For example: myself, Accipter, TQ (I think) and any 'weak' atheist; the two terms are synonymous.
            </li>
           </ul>
           <br/>
           <br/>
           Thus, as someone who isn't sure but chooses not to believe, Accipter is an agnostic atheist.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           EDIT: It's true that 'agnostic' is often taken to be
           <i>
            about
           </i>
           atheism because it's where the word is most commonly used - hence your definition. However, being an agnostic and being an atheist are by no means mutually exclusive - and being 'an agnostic' does not prevent you from being a theist.

               <br/>
               <br/>
               There are two dichotomies being discussed here:
               <br/>
               (a)Theism - whether you believe in a God or not.
               <br/>
               (a)Gnositicism - whether you (think you) know something or not.
               <br/>
               <br/>
               The thing about dichotomies is that you cannot ignore them - you have to fall one side or the other. You can't be just 'an agnostic' - you have to be 'an agnostic'
               <i>
                about
               </i>
               something (
               <a href="http://www.businessweek.com/news/2010-02-11/obama-agnostic-on-deficit-cuts-won-t-prejudge-tax-increases.html" target="_blank">

               </a>
               ). Simple maths, then, dictates that you have a combination of four possibles to choose from:
               <br/>
               <br/>
               <ul>
                <li style="">
                 Gnostic theist - one who believes in God, and is certain of it. For example: those who claim to have had a 'personal experience'.
                </li>
                <li style="">
                 Agnostic theist - one who believes in God, but cannot be sure. For example: who were born into the faith and don't see any reason to change.
                </li>
                <li style="">
                 Gnostic atheist - one who does not believe in God, and is fully confident in this lack of belief. For example: Richard Dawkins, Christopher Hitchens and any 'strong' atheist; the terms are synonymous.
                </li>
                <li style="">
                 Agnostic atheist - one who does not believe in God, but accepts that they may be wrong. For example: myself, Accipter, TQ (I think) and any 'weak' atheist; the two terms are synonymous.
                </li>
               </ul>
               <br/>
               <br/>
               Thus, as someone who isn't sure but chooses not to believe, Accipter is an agnostic atheist.
               <br/>
               <br/>
               EDIT: It's true that 'agnostic' is often taken to be
               <i>
                about
               </i>
               atheism because it's where the word is most commonly used - hence your definition. However, being an agnostic and being an atheist are by no means mutually exclusive - and being 'an agnostic' does not prevent you from being a theist.
              </div>
             </div>
            </div>
