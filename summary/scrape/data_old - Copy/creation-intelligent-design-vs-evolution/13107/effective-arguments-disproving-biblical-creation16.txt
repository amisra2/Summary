              <div class="message">
               But have you ever seen man turn a LIZARD into a DOG?
               <br/>
               <br/>
               I think not. Nor will you see it. That is what macro-evolution is claiming what led to your existence, and evolution does claim it happened in reality in that before dogs were dogs, they were first reptiles which evolved from amphibians which evolved from fish and so on going back to proteins and bacteria in the primordial ooze.
              </div>
             </div>
            </div>
           </div>
           -sigh-
           <br/>
           <br/>
           Archie, I'm sorry, but this is just wrong. No evolutionist has
           <i>
            ever
           </i>
           claimed that lizards can turn into dogs.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           What is claimed is that dogs evolved from wolves. That wolves themselves evolved from other wolf-like carnivores. That wolf-carnivores evolved from other carnivores. That carnivores evolved from other placental mammals. That placental mammals evolved from earlier egg-laying mammals. And that egg-laying mammals evolved from a group of reptiles that were very similar to mammals in a number of respects (e.g. jaw and ear structure, dentition, legs and locomotion, etc.).
           <br/>
           <br/>
           There are also a number of ways in which these so-called "mammal-like reptiles" superficially bear more resemblance to modern lizards than to modern mammals. But the mammal-like reptiles are emphatically not lizards, and this is about as close as you're going to get to a lizards-to-dogs scenario.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           <div class="bbcode_container">
            <div class="bbcode_quote">
             <div class="quote_container">
              <div class="bbcode_quote_container">
              </div>
              ...every diverse type and variety of life on Earth today originally came from the same single celled protein which was somehow born into and survived in a molten lava environment of helium and sulfuric acid long enough to grow, replicate, and over a couple of billions more years in that environment until the earth slowly cooled, and life somehow formed into more complex organisms.
             </div>
            </div>
           </div>
           Again -- huh? A couple billion years of "a molten lava environment of helium and sulfuric acid?" I don't think so. Where are you getting this stuff?
           <br/>
           <br/>
           <div class="bbcode_container">
            <div class="bbcode_quote">
             <div class="quote_container">
              <div class="bbcode_quote_container">
              </div>
              And for Gods sake please personally educate yourself regarding this subject...
             </div>
            </div>
           </div>
           Right back atcha!
           <br/>
           <br/>
           <br/>
           <div class="bbcode_container">
            <div class="bbcode_quote">
             <div class="quote_container">
              <div class="bbcode_quote_container">
              </div>
              [Citation:] Nobel laureate Francis Crick[/B] calculated natures chances of producing one small protein: 1 in 10 to the 260th power. Crick reminds us there are only 10 to the 80th power (1 followed by 80 zeros) atoms in the whole universe; he concludes even the elementary components of life "cannot have arisen by pure chance."
             </div>
            </div>
           </div>
           Indeed it cannot. And what you are ignoring is that
           <i>
            natural selection is not pure chance
           </i>
           .
           <br/>
           <br/>
           Crick himself came to realize that his earlier views of abiogenesis were wrong. Here's a link:
           <a href="http://www.fasebj.org/cgi/reprint/7/1/238.pdf" target="_blank">
            http://www.fasebj.org/cgi/reprint/7/1/238.pdf
           </a>
           .
           <br/>
           <br/>
           <div class="bbcode_container">
            <div class="bbcode_quote">
             <div class="quote_container">
              <div class="bbcode_quote_container">
              </div>
              [Citation:] Sir Fred Hoyle calculated natures chances of producing the 2000 enzymes found in life: 1 in 10 to the 40,000th power. He states: "The Darwinian theory of evolution is shown to be plainly wrong." He concludes: "Life cannot have had a random beginning(16)...but must have come from a cosmic intelligence."
             </div>
            </div>
           </div>
           Hoyle's understanding of Darwinian evolution is the only thing that is "plainly wrong." To reword yet repeat,
           <i>
            natural selection is not random
           </i>
           .
           <br/>
           <br/>
           <div class="bbcode_container">
            <div class="bbcode_quote">
             <div class="quote_container">
              <div class="bbcode_quote_container">
              </div>
              [Citation:] Nobel laureate Ernst Chain said: "To postulate that the development and survival of the fittest is entirely a consequence of chance mutations seems to me a hypothesis based on no evidence and irreconciliable with the facts."
             </div>
            </div>
           </div>
           Chain is absolutely right: Life did not develop and diversify "
           <i>
            entirely
           </i>
           [as] a consequence of chance mutations." He left out
           <i>
            natural selection
           </i>
           .
           <br/>
           <br/>
           It is time to retire the "life can't have originated or diversified randomly" non-criticism. Evolution doesn't propose that it did.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           To say otherwise indicates only that there is a flaw in the speaker's understanding of evolution, not that there is a flaw in the theory of evolution itself.
          </blockquote>
