           </div>
           This is off-topic, but I'd just like to point out that the folk wisdom that there are exceptions to every rule is incoherent.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           Let's see why by examining the formal structure of the claim "There are exceptions to every rule". This can be taken to say that for any rule, whatever it may be, there is an exception to it.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           Saying that there is an exception to every rule however is formally congruent to saying there is a goose for every group of chickens. If we gather a group of chickens such that we discover that we have also gathered a goose, it is more accurate to say that we have made an error in how we gather chickens than make a weak inductive claim that all groups of chickens include a goose.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           A rule either covers all of the objects to which it pertains, or fails to be a well-applied rule. Any 'exception' to a rule is outside of the scope of such a rule.  'Exceptions' merely show that you are applying a rule incorrectly. When you are applying a rule correctly, there are no exceptions.
          </blockquote>
         </div>
