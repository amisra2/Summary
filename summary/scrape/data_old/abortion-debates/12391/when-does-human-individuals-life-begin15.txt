              Most in this thread have already acknowledged the fact that a human individual's life begins "biologically" at the moment of their conception, AO. If you can prove otherwise,... now's your chance!
             </div>
            </div>
           </div>
           ...the times I've done this, you've ignored my posts. I have done it twice, now.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           Collecting past responses in a third attempt (and adding lots more - see part 1)... here goes nothing.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           1) Of all the words you've linked too as 'suggested reading' in your OP, you haven't included the most important one: 'life'.
           <br/>
           Probably the most relevant definition from
           <a href="http://dictionary.reference.com/browse/life?qsrc=2888" target="_blank">
            dictionary.com
           </a>
           defines 'life' as '
           <i>
            the animate existence or period of animate existence of an individual
           </i>
           '. Something alive also manifests '
           <i>
            the power of adaptation to environment through changes originating internally
           </i>
           '. Neither of these features match a zygote.
           <br/>
           <a href="http://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/life" target="_blank">
            Merriam-Webster
           </a>
           goes one step further, it states that 'life' is '
           <i>
            5a: the period from birth to death
           </i>
           '. The
           <a href="http://www.merriam-webster.com/medical/life" target="_blank">
            medical M-R
           </a>
           includes the definitions '
           <i>
            1 a: the quality that distinguishes a vital and functional plant or animal from a dead body
           </i>
           ' - we've been over this before in one of my first posts in this thread: in humans ('human individuals', even), this distinguishing feature is brain activity. Another excerpt from the medical M-R: '
           <i>
            2 a: the sequence of physical and mental experiences that make up the existence of an individual
           </i>
           '. Note '
           <i>
            experiences
           </i>
           ' (and 'mental'). A zygote does not experience anything.
           <br/>
           <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#Definitions" target="_blank">
            Wikipedia
           </a>
           also throws a wrench into your idea that life has an absolute scientific definition: '
           <i>
            There is no universal definition of life. To define life in unequivocal terms is still a challenge for scientists
           </i>
           '.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           1.5) More definition-based stuff.
           <a href="http://dictionary.reference.com/browse/birth?qsrc=2888" target="_blank">
            birth
           </a>
           , from your own link, includes the definition '
           <i>
            any coming into existence
           </i>
           '. This isn't a massively strong line of argument - but it just highlights the fact that dictionary wars are kinda pointless.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           2) We've discussed previously how an 'individual' must be 'independent' - that is, they must be able to exist without the external aid of another organism. The fact that a zygote is entirely reliant on external aid for it's continued existence (as more than a collection of molecules - to be as per
           <b>
            s
           </b>
           nickety as to argue this is to include, for example, ice as capable of 'existence' in a fire, because the water molecules are still present) kinda outrules it's 'individuality'.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           3) At conception, the only thing that happens is the occurrence of unique DNA - the cells were alive both before and after this point. This does not, however, mean that a unique individual will be formed as a result - nor does a unique individual have to be the result of a single conception. See large parts of the
           <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Twin#Complications_of_twin_pregnancy" target="_blank">
            twins
           </a>
           wiki article - identical twins share the same DNA and the same conception point - are they the same 'individual'? Turning it on it's head, a 'chimera' organism (again, see the wiki article) has different parts with different DNA - one 'individual' can result from
           <i>
            two
           </i>
           conceptions. Does this individual have two 'lives'?
           <br/>
           <br/>
           To sum up: you are claiming that a fertilised egg is a 'newly alive human individual'. I agree that the zygote is 'human'; but that's about as far as it goes. The
           <i>
            cell(s)
           </i>
           making up a zygote are 'alive' - but the sperm and egg that made up a zygote were just as alive; there is no 'newly' if you wish to use this form of the definition. The zygote is also not an 'individual' - it is dependent on other organisms for it's continued existence, and it may directly turn into either two (or more) individuals, or end up as a mere fraction of a complete individual.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           I'd also add, for the [repetitive]'th time, that an 'individual' in the
           <i>
            biological
           </i>
           sense is not synonymous with an 'individual' in the
           <i>
            social
           </i>
           sense.
