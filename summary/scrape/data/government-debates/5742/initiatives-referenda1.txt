          <blockquote class="postcontent restore">
           Roughly 24 states in the U.S.—predominantly western—have a system of initiative or referendum amendments on their books which allow voters to vote on a variety of issues in a straight up or down matter, often during general elections. These have become wildly popular of late, it seems to me anyway, and it is difficult to find any criticism of the practice in the media. I would like to offer some here.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           Of course, it is our right to have this power, and it is enumerated in many of our states’ Constitutions. My contention is that we’re exercising it too vigorously.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           I have been guided by a simple principle in government: If a thing is good for the stability of the government, I’m for it. If it tends to disrupt the stability I’m against it. You could say I’m a Big-Stability-in-Government guy. Initiatives and Referenda (IR) tend to disrupt stability in a huge way, IMO.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           We are a Constitutional Republic, not a democracy, and I believe we should act accordingly. We elect our representatives to vote in our interests, and it is this removal and critical distance from the clash and spark of politics which has allowed us to outfit a strong keel on our ship of state.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           Some people, maddened by slow or non-existent “progress” attempt to take matters into their own hands and so submit an idea for an IR, garnering signatures on petitions in an always populist fashion, doing an end-run around our politicians. In this way, modern America is beginning to resemble Athenian democracy, sheer mob rule.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           I think it’s a sorry turn of events. I would like to hear what you think.
          </blockquote>

           I think your objections to the promiscuous use of referenda are intelligent and well argued. In general, perhaps only in consitutional issues or matters of great national consequence, should the "sovereign will" of the people be directly consulted by a referendum. Elections of representatives are perfectly adequate for almost every other political contingency.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           However, the United States is a special case. It's a  continental union of 50 (?) states with a lot of power devolved or delegated from the center. It's a long way, for example, from Alaska to Washington DC, and the federal government is probably seen as remote and out of touch with the "pressing concerns of the people". You might be able to influence a policy determined in St Paul, Minnesota, but you could make no difference to the "Washington view", no matter how much noise you make. To many, perhaps most people, the state is a more meaningful political abstraction than the concept of a far away federal government. For these sorts of reasons, the use of referenda has been conceived, I think, as a way of "connecting people to politics" - i.e. to the state administration.
           <br/>
