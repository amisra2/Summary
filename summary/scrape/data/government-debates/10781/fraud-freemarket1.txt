             </div>

           How can you "drive out of business" someone whose business consists of a crapload of oil in storage, the quantity of which you most likely aren't even aware of?  Their ongoing costs are slim to none, and the potential profits they can make by holding out enormous.  Your task, monopoly-theory investor, is to buy as much oil up as you can at the last minute before the price goes up.  You can wait as long as you want to start, the more accurate your prediction of the time of the price rise, the greater your profit, and the greater the loss Standard Oil suffers.  How long can it maintain its discount rates, 10 years, 100, 1000?

           <br/>
           <br/>
           That's the worst business model I ever heard of, and no one with the commercial sense of Rockefeller would have the stupidity to pursure it.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           What's worse to make this whole scheme work properly, Standard Oil has to adequately supply the market with all the fuel it needs, even at the low rates it has set.  If it runs out of oil, people buy from its competitors, even at higher prices.  To sustain full market control at low price levels, Standard Oil would need to continually expand its industrial base, its logistics and refining capacities.   And then what happens when it starts charging vastly more for it and demand slips?  Burn its old assets or let them rot?  Sell them off, and introduce into the market competitors who can easily compete due to low costs?  The whole enterprise is an adventure in how to ruin your business.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           So yes, a business with the size of Standard Oil can successfully, but temporarily, bone consumers, if that is its goal.  But it can only do so by ruining its own commercial prospects to the benefit of the consumer.  If the goal of Standard Oil is to make profit, it should not go down this path.  And no one ever accused a capitalist of being insufficiently greedy.
