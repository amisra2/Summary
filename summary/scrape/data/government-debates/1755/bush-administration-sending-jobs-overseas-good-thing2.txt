          <blockquote class="postcontent restore">
           Hmmm.. had a similiar discussion about this at another site. Here are my thoughts on "job export overseas":
           <br/>
           <br/>
           Manual labour loss to the overseas can indeed be good. It is a good way to make the labour force more efficient, and it also serves to be able to purchase cheaper goods. In the end it will lead to an increase in wages for manual labour the capital/labourer ratio will move in favour for the workers (assumning that the unemployment will dissipate, which is questionable).
           <br/>
           <br/>
           But this is assuming that the american capital will stay in the US. If the capital moves elsewhere, there will be no efficiency increase in the US. There will be no wage increase as the capital/labour ratio will stay as it where. In fact, the overall capital/labour ratio will drop (ie less capital). This will effect the overall wage rate negatively. But there will still be cheaper goods of course (possibly negating the wage rate drop), but who will be better off?
           <br/>
           <br/>
           While the overall american wellfare may rise (asuming that the unemployment will dissipate and that the new imports doesn`t raise the trade decifit) the workers will probably not be better off, they will most probably be worse off. The public will perhaps be better off (due to cheaper goods) but how much better off they will become is relative to their income (that is; the more you earn, the "more" better off you will be by this change). And of course the capital owners will be better off (otherwise they wouldn`t have moved their capital).
           <br/>
           <br/>
           Now, you see that there are a lot of assumptions here... If for exammple unemployment does not disipate it will be a costly affair, and if the trade decifit is made even worse it could become a problem for future generation americans. But it could be just fine and dandy as the overall change would probably be positive (providing the assumptions are correct). But if nothing is done to re-distribute the gains it will basicly lead to some get shafted and some is better off.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           But then we have the political implications of globally transferable capital and what it does to the democratic self-determination of the nation state.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           It is clear that a nations economic wealth/capita is heavily dependant on the capital/labour ratio. It is the amount of capital put into production (both humane capital and machines) that determines the productivity per worker. And as the productivity per worker decides GNP/capita a decrease in capital/labour ratio will effect the GNP/capita negativly.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           Now, that would mean that any government, who is concerned with the wellbeing of its citizens should maximize capital accumulation. This is pretty straightforward and could be done in many ways. But, most governments also have certain political aspirations (for example; minimum wages, social security net, defense, right to join labour unions etc), and these political aspirations will both cost money and most probable will have negative effect on the capital owners (as they are paying taxes on their capital, and seldom need to use these services). In a closed system, this will only have marginal effects on the capital accumulation. In an open system it will have much more effect as the capital can be moved to another location where these political aspirations are lower (or non-existant). And as the international system is one of anarchy, it will lead to competition between states over easy moveable capital (ie; a reduction in the political aspirations, or a reduction in the ability for a nations public to decide democraticly how their country should be like).
           <br/>
           <br/>
           So in essence, job flight overseas does not nearly pose as a serious problem (assuming alot of things) as capital flight and the political implications of a globally transferable capital.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           Phew, sorry for the rant...
