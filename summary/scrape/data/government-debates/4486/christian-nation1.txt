          <blockquote class="postcontent restore">
           My first inclination was to ask for clarification. Does the question ask about whether many or most citizens of the U.S. are Christians, or is the question about whether our government is somehow based on or is it motivated by Christian principles.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           It seems that the question is more like the latter, only should the United States affirm Christianity or religion in general.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           Well, the writers of the Constitution were well aware of the religious wars among Christians in Europe. It was quite evident that when religion became a function of government, that it became more and more imporant which particular sect was considered the "correct" true religion. Even the writers of the Constitution held several different faiths, depending on from which state they came. They were very aware of the religious persecutions that were carried out in the American colonies. They realized the problems that would arise in trying to form a strong federal government that recognized a single state religion - which one? So they avoided the issue by forming a government that said nothing about religion and didn't mention God. The authority of the Constitution was derived from "We the people...," and not from divine authority or holy scripture. In fact, the Godlessness of the Constitution was one of the reasons that the Constitution was openly opposed by most Christian churches, with the exception of the Baptists who realized that a government formed without a religious basis would be to their benefit - no federal taxes to finance religions that persecuted them.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           In these debates, those who claim that the U.S. was founded on Christian principles always point to the Declaration of Independence. They see their religion in the expressions used there, usually because they are willing to distort the meaning of the text. For example, in the first paragraph, Jefferson used the word "God." However, in context it is clear that he was not refering to the Christian God. Jefferson declares that events have made it necessary for the people to assume a separate and equal station among the powers of earth and that it is the "Laws of Nature and of Nature's God" that entitle them to this separation. Such references to nature were common in humanist writing and "God" is given second billing, being subject to the same laws that control nature. Also note that it is a respect to the opinions of mankind that motivates the declaration, not a duty to any god.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           In the next paragraph, Jefferson stated certain unalienable rights. Beyond the fact that the rights were considered to be self evident (not a matter of revelation by scripture), unalienable rights are not principles of Christianity anywhere. Individual rights have always been considered subject to the needs of the church. And of course, the statement of the equality of all men is intended as a direct response to the Christian idea that someone is, "by the Grace of God, King." And of course, governments derive their powers from the consent of the governed, not from anyone's god, and the governed retain the right to change that government.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           But all of the above discussion is actually moot. The Declaration is not a founding document of this country. It is what it says it is, and nothing more - a declaration. Our first government was actually founded the the Articles of Confederation. When this government was recognized to be inadequate to the future needs of the country, the Continental Congress made itself the Constitutional Convention and assigned itself the task of writing a new Constitution.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           But even the "Christian nation" advocates are aware that God is not mentioned once in the Constitution. Instead it is "We the People" who "ordain and establish" the Constitution. Including the Bill of Rights, Jesus and God are not mentioned once, and religion is mentioned twice, both times in the form of prohibitions.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           So no, the United States is not a Christian nation.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           There is also the matter of the Treaty of Peace and Friendship between the United States and the Bey and Subjects of Tripoli of Barbary (known as the Treaty of Tripoli). This treaty was negotiated and written by Joel Barlow during Washington's 2nd term. Barlow had previously been appointed as Chaplain of the Continental Army by Washington. The treaty was approved by the Senate without a single dissenting vote and signed by President Adams. You may see some discussion that article 11 is not included in the Arabic version of this document and that that fact is somehow telling. However, the version ratified by the Senate and signed by Adams said the following:
           <br/>
           "As the Government of the United States of America is not, in any sense, founded on the Christian religion;"
           <br/>
           Keep in mind that the year was 1797 - the country was just over 8 years old. One might think that the Senate would be well aware of the intent of the founding fathers, especially since some of them were the founding fathers.
          </blockquote>
