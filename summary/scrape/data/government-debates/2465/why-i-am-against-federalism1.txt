          <blockquote class="postcontent restore">
           In the 1980's, I became a member of the Reagan Revolution, which sought to limit the size and scope of the federal government and return power to the individual states. This, oddly enough, is called federalism. It had at its core a very appealing message to me: States are more responsible with our tax dollars and are in a better position to determine what is best for its citizens, as opposed to sending money to the out-of-touch politicians in Washington, D.C. presiding over a bloated budget. The movement has continued to the present, where Republicans today continue to push the "devolution revolution", wanting more and more power devolved to the local level, such as school boards, cities and townships.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           Opposing them are the people who believe in a strong federal government which can best watch over the interests of all people. Even more odder, this could be called statism.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           This very divided country has had me examining my beliefs closely of late and I'm beginning to become very disenchanted with federalism. Here's my thinking.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           I don't believe we could ever have become a great nation, striving to give equal rights to all, or provide some common sense measures to protect the environment for instance, with federalism. I find pettiness creeping in at a local level, a way for small-time politicians to push their small-minded agendas. The township supervisors where I live have hardly shed their Neanderthal brows yet, let alone debate the pros and cons of the space program. There is something comforting to travel from state to state and see more uniformity than not. I could easily imagine check stops at each border, being given thick booklets containing myriads of local laws and rules, all the result of some pet project of local politicos.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           Questions:
           <br/>
           <br/>
           1. Do I have my terminology right?
           <br/>
           2. Am I becoming a liberal weenie?
           <br/>
           3. Can one hold a view like this and still be called a fiscal conservative?
           <br/>
           4. Where am I going wrong in my thinking?
           <br/>
           <br/>
           Thanks.
          </blockquote>

           I think the concept of federalism went (and still goes) much further than simply fiscal control.  It is about the sharing of power between a centralised (the federal) and decentralised (the states) government.  The idea is not only that people at the state level are more adept at deciding how money should be spent, but also that the states should have more power.  If citizens of a state become dissatisfied with their government, they could change it in 3 or 4 years, completely sweeping house and installing new people to all elected positions.  This extends to things like law enforcement as well.  Our local sheriffs department has a conviction rate that makes the FBI look downright incompetent.  The thing is, MOST sheriffs departments in my state have conviction rates that make the FBI look incompetent.  The exceptions are out west, in counties that only have a hundred or so people, and have no crime.  THey have a conviction rate of either 0 or 100%, depending on how you look at it.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           They manage this because your local sheriffs department is right there on the ground, every day.  They know the people, they know what is going on, and they know who to ask if something is going on that shouldn't be.  THey treat the people around here like citizens with rights, because otherwise the sheriff will not be re-elected and he knows it, so he keeps his guys on a leash.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           At a  federal level, the agents don't have the advatages that the local sheriffs department does.  We try to make up for this with more and cooler toys and more power for our LE personell.  IT doesn't usually work.  They also do not have to play nice nearly so much, since they are buffered from the court of public opinion.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           In my state we have state welfar and social programs.  They run without the waste that comes with the federal system.  Over 80% of the money that goes into the system ends up helping poor people, with the other 20% running to administraive costs.  When was the last time a federal social program ate up less than half the money on administrative costs?  If I remember correctly, it was during the depression.

The government in our coutry ate up only 5% pf the GDP, as opposed to over 50% today.  People didn;t recieve as much education in general, but te education they did recieve was of a much higher quality.  I read a report not long ago that a member of our local school board had prepared.  They took some old tests (circa 1940) and administered them to a bunch of graduating seniors from one of our local high schools.  NOt a single one passed the test.  This is pathetic.  We are supposed to be getting SMARTER.

We also have politicians pushing small minded agendas and being petty at a national level, and they are MUCH harder to get rid of.

           This was the genius of the American system.  We were suposed to have the best of both worlds.  You have local control over many things, programs being run directly within sight of people who are able to pull the plug if it doesn't seem to be working out, and politicians who are also operating under the scrutiny of citizens who can remove them from office easily if they fail to perform.  This is democracy.  Our founding fathers apparently believed in the wisdom of the population at large to keep such things from happening.  Perhaps they were mistaken, becasue our population at large does not seem to be exibitng a lot of wisdom lately.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           We also have a federal system in place, that does things like handle space programs and make sure that all the different states play nice.  They also act as a check on the powers of the state and ensure our rights are recognised by those states.

           IMHO you are seeing too many things as black or white.  There are many shades of grey.  Federalism does not preclude the federal government from operating things like the space program, or the military.  It simply shifts some of the power, and some of the burden of governing, to the states.  Traditionally, federalists talk about things like shifting social programs to the states.  From my experience, this is a good idea.  Any time you run any agency, there is going to be waste.  In a huge system like the federal welfare program, these are going to be HUGE wastes.  In a smaller program such as a state might run, the program is likely to be run far more efficiently just for the fact that it is easier to oversee a smaller program.
           <br/>

YOu still need a federal LE agency to handle crimes that take place or involve many different jurisdictions, but the majority of crimes do not fall into such a category.  Somebody is going to say that "we need a large federal LE frce becasue local departments don't have the funding or equipment to handle it on their own".  That person will be wrong.  Compare conviction rates of local LE agencies to federal agencies and this becomes quickly apparent.  Pick a random local LE agency if you want.  I don;t think it really matter which one you pick.
