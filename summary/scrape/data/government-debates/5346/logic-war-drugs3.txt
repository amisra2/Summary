           That seems to be question-begging as I understand it. If what you're saying is that "You are satisfied" really means "You have the feeling of satisfaction", then it seems that "You have the feeling of satisfaction" really means "You have the feeling that you feel satisfaction". This proposition now seems to really mean: "You have the feeling that you feel the feeling of satisfaction". And so on.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           That's why I tried to suggest we phrase these propositions differently, so that they represent more accurately what I am trying to say.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           Instead of saying "I am satisfied" or some variation, it seems more accurate to say that "My interests are satisfied." and "I am feeling
           <i>
            satisfied
           </i>
           (1) that my interests are
           <i>
            satisfied
           </i>
           (3)" to properly differentiate between the thing to which satisfaction in the first sense pertains and the perception to which satisfaction in the second sense pertains.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           It might be something to differentiate between the two of the uses of satisfaction to avoid amphiboly, eh?
           <br/>
           <br/>
           <a href="http://dictionary.reference.com/search?q=satisfaction" target="_blank">
            http://dictionary.reference.com/search?q=satisfaction
           </a>
           <br/>
           <br/>
           Specifically we appear to be dealing with #1 and #3, and #3 is poorly worded so that it seems to imply perception whereas I'm trying to stress a 'satisfaction' in terms of satisfying conditions.
           <br/>
           <div class="bbcode_container">

           They were intended to demonstrate that first order satisfaction is not a feeling in the #1 sense of 'satisfaction'. Let me try again:
           <br/>
           <br/>
           1. Suppose that an abstracted set of interests are either satisfied or not according to some scheme of necessary and sufficient conditions such that each normative statement of interests is matched by a state of the world.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           2. Suppose further that the state of the world must be interpreted or perceived such that each state of the world may be matched by a descriptive statement.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           3. If the descriptive statement derived from perception of the world states appear to match the normative statements defining the interests, then we will feel satisfied by this turn of events because it appears that our interests are satisfied.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           4. Therefore regardless of how we fail to satisfy our interests, we may feel satisfaction when our interests are not satisfied.
           <br/>
           <br/>
