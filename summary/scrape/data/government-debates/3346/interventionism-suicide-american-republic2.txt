           A few responses. I think the report of
           <a href="http://www.hrw.org/wr2k4/3.htm" target="_blank">
            Human Rights Watch
           </a>
           on humaniterean intereventions has some important things to say on this topic (full disclosure: in spite of the below quote, HRW still opposed the intervention in Iraq for reasons explored in the article. For my thoughts and counter-analysis to that, see
           <a href="http://dsadevil.blogspot.com/2004/10/humaniterean-intervention.html" target="_blank">
            here
           </a>
           ). They write:
           <div class="bbcode_container">
            <div class="bbcode_quote">
             <div class="quote_container">
              <div class="bbcode_quote_container">
              </div>
              [I]t is worth noting two factors that we do not consider relevant in assessing whether an intervention can be justified as humanitarian. First, we are aware of, but reject, the argument that humanitarian intervention cannot be justified if other equally or more needy places are ignored. Iraqi repression was severe, but the case might be made that repression elsewhere was worse. For example, an estimated three million or more have lost their lives to violence, disease, and exposure in recent years during the conflict in the eastern Democratic Republic of Congo (DRC), yet intervention in the DRC was late and, compared to Iraq, modest. However, if the killing in Iraq warranted military intervention, it would be callous to disregard the plight of these victims simply because other victims were being neglected. In that case, intervention should be encouraged in both places, not rejected in one because it was weak or nonexistent in the other.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              Second, we are aware of, but reject, the argument that past U.S. complicity in Iraqi repression should preclude U.S. intervention in Iraq on humanitarian grounds. This argument is built on the U.S. government’s sordid record in Iraq in the 1980s and early 1990s. When the Iraqi government was using chemical weapons against Iranian troops in the 1980s, the Reagan administration was giving it intelligence information. After the Anfal genocide against Iraqi Kurds in 1988, the Reagan and first Bush administrations gave Baghdad billions of dollars in commodity credits and import loan guarantees. The Iraqi government’s ruthless suppression of the 1991 uprising was facilitated by the first Bush administration’s agreement to Iraq’s use of helicopters – permission made all the more callous because then-President Bush had encouraged the uprising in the first place. In each of these cases, Washington deemed it more important to defeat Iran or avoid Iranian influence in a potentially destabilized Iraq than to discourage or prevent large-scale slaughter. We condemn such calculations. However, we would not deny relief to, say, the potential victims of genocide simply because the proposed intervener had dirty hands in the past.
             </div>
            </div>
           </div>
           Yes, its certainly true that in all likelihood, the repression in NK or Congo was worse than the repression in Iraq. But for a myriad of geopolitical and national-political reasons, Iraqi intervention was what was on the table, and to oppose it on the grounds that it shunted forces away from interventions that WEREN'T GOING TO HAPPEN REGARDLESS is foolish and counter-intuitive. This is a seperate argument, of course, for the claim that it shunted forces away from the war on terror as a whole; to that argument, I would say that only when the US proves to the Arab world that we are as concerned with improving Muslim lives as we are with ending them can we reverse the tide of ethnic hatred and resentment that fuels terrorism in that region. By working to democratize Iraq, and hopefully by extension democratize the region, we show that our interest in the Middle East extends beyond economic and security concerns, and recognizes them as "real" people who are also worthy of moral worth and respect as human beings.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           The other thing I'd like to briefly discuss is the differences between Iraq and Vietnam. There is certainly one MUCH bigger difference beyond the checking effect of China. That difference is that in Iraq, the US fought a war against tyranny in order to establish a democracy, while in Vietnam we fought a war against democracy in order to prop up a tyranny. I've written about this before, the US KNEW that national hero Ho Chi Minh would easily win any free and fair election in the country and thus refused to allow treaty-mandated elections that were slated to occur in the country. It was that action which perpetuated the division of the country into "North" and "South" and created the groundwork for war, in addition to enabling the oppressive tendancies of the southern vietnamese government. Since the US was correctly seen as a colonialist presence serving as a BARRIER to the free will of the Vietnamese people, it's no wonder that we failed to win the hearts and minds of the local populace. In Iraq, by example, our motives, if not pure, at least were not tainted by an overt opposition to Democracy, which is a key difference insofar as it makes it at least objectively possible that we could convince the Iraqi people of our benign intentions, notwithstanding the fact that Bush has messed up so badly that our window of oppurtunity might be irrevocably lost.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           Finally, I'm aware that our fiscal problems extend beyond foolish tax cuts. The original analysis still stands, however. Insofar as we have to set priorities in terms of where we reduce spending, it seems unfair and deeply immoral to sacrifice the most oppressed and least powerful in order to provide for the relatively wealthy and better off in the global sphere. When weighing oppurtunity costs of spending cuts, we should view it through a prism that makes sure we don't further isolate the marginalized. It seems clear to me that we can restructure our domestic spending actions such that we could still fulfill our moral obligations without facing an economic collapse, especially since the ideal end-result of these FP choices would be to further liberalize the world, which gives the US more markets to trade in and would, longterm, boost economic growth than what we have in the status quo.
          </blockquote>
