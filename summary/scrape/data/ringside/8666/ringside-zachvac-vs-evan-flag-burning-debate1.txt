           Well, Round 1 is in the books, and I must say I'm a bit disappointed.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           zachvac had a difficult task, as his opponent's approach was unknown at the time of posting.  It seemed to me that he danced around the issue a bit before simply declaring that the Founding Fathers intended a blanket freedom of expression and insisting that "offensive" activities need to be protected.  While I happen to agree with the former (and generally the latter), I think zachvac has not supported his case particularly well; perhaps Evan will put some pressure on him in this respect.
           <br/>
           I would have liked to have seen references to judicial rulings, Founding Fathers' statements, or citations of the Constitution in support of his point.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           Evan, on the other hand, took an approach that caught me totally by surprise... not that I feel that will get him much.  First, if I'm not mistaken, the understanding was that the "constitution" referenced in the resolution is the U.S. Constitution, which means that appeals to Canadian Code is at best tangetially useful in this debate.  Nevertheless, Evan seems to spend a lot of time on the Canadian Code.
           <br/>
           Evan's definition of "violence" leaves much to be desired for this context, and is ripe for the picking (apart) by zachvac, in my opinion.
           <br/>
           Additionally, Evan's incomplete citation of Section 433 of the Criminal Code of Canada led me to wonder what was left out.  While I'll refrain from elaborating on this during the debate (as I'm not a participant), I feel that Evan is misrepresenting the statute and its intent, although the semantics are somewhat entertaining.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           Again, I fear that Evan may be handcuffed by the resolution and will have a tough time finding sources that support his resolved position, but -- again -- any case law he might be able to find would be helpful.  However, it is my opinion that -- unless the debate is about Canadian law -- Evan needs to focus on the U.S.
           <br/>
           I think zachvac can hit the majority of Evan's response out of the park; however, the burden of proof is still upon zachvac as the affirmant to support his point.  He could rebut Evan all day long and still lose the debate because he hasn't adequately argued his position.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           My take: Evan is fractionally ahead, even if only mostly by default.  He is vulnerable, but even a "draw" would result in a victory for the negative side.
           <br/>
           <br/>
           Perhaps we'll see some heavier action in Round 2.
